# Client-Server Scalability

**Authors:** Tam Vo, Brendan Nichols, Craig Beaubien 

## Introduction 
Our research focus is on the Client/Server Application architecture with an emphasis on large-scale scalable design and distributed algorithms for communication and coordination. Our project will center around utilizing AWS Kinesis Data Streams as a managed middleware solution to connect the high volume of client data with a cluster of backend servers. We will investigate how a distributed system can be designed to handle unpredictable traffic spikes while maintaining a high Quality of Service (QoS) for the end user.  

Distributed high-frequency user telemetry data (mouse movements, vertical/horizontal scrolling patterns, and sub-second clickstreams) via an e-commerce website will be the centerpiece of our research. Real-time insights like these are becoming a common pattern to provide immediate feedback for product designers to identify user experience obstacles, refine web analytics in real-time, and so much more. This, however, becomes a challenge in distributed systems design, specifically regarding reliability and scalability of data ingestion and middleware processing. 

Through our review of existing literature, we found that “exactly-once” delivery behavior does not hold true for high-throughput systems, so instead we will shift towards an “at-least-once" delivery combined with idempotent processing. Additionally, we discovered that horizontal scaling remains the industry standard for throughput management, but the overhead during dynamic re-sharding can introduce latency spikes that can cause problems for the real-time nature of heat map generation. Our survey paper summarizes this foundational research and will lay the groundwork for the rest of our project. 

## Literature Review  
Modern distributed systems must be scalable across three critical dimensions: numerical, geographical, and administrative [1]. Numerical scale is the system’s ability to handle an increasing number of users and objects without performance degradation. Geographical scale is the system's ability to address the challenges of network latency as nodes are dispersed across wide-area networks. Administrative scale refers to the task of managing systems across vastly different organizational domains specifically where trust and security may not be amicable.  

For our areas of focus, we are exploring distribution and replication strategies to address numerical scalability, distributed middleware to address geographical scalability, and AWS cloud infrastructure to address administrative scalability. AWS cloud infrastructure requires robust authentication and policy-defined access for Kinesis Data Stream. We will be testing Neuman’s central thesis for scalability to see whether raw power or decentralized management with the reduction of synchronous coordination overhead succeeds.  

With a big part of our project focusing on how much load our server can take, and managing the throughput of client data, it was important to understand the current research on autoscaling techniques through Kubernetes so that we could scale up our clients and increase load. Kubernetes already offers methods for Horizontal Pod Autoscaling and Vertical Pod Autoscaling, but these default options can be improved upon. After reading a paper by Tran et al. [4] which went over current autoscaling methods and improvements being researched in the area, we found that there are many different factors to consider when deciding how to scale a project. For starters, it is important to decide based on your performance requirements whether you want to scale your project horizontally, vertically, or even use a hybrid of both. You must also consider what metric you want to base your scaling from, which was explored by A. Khaleq and I. Ra in [6]. In their paper, they found that dynamically scaling a system based on its characteristics and resource requirements was better than Kubernetes’ default horizontal scaling algorithm.  

Some interesting approaches on autoscaling that we found throughout our research were attempts at hybrid scaling, a combination of both vertical and horizontal autoscaling. As stated by Tran et al. in [4], with just horizontal scaling you can typically maintain Quality-of-Service but will often end up underutilizing resources. With vertical scaling, you can more efficiently use CPU and memory resources but will often struggle to meet performance requirements as the workload spikes. In the face of these drawbacks, the goal of hybrid autoscaling is to address the shortcomings of using either method on its own and therefore provide a better and more efficient scaling method. 

An example of this hybrid scaling method, authored by Vu et al. [5], proposed a predictive method of hybrid scaling. By utilizing machine learning, specifically a type of Recurrent Neural Network which can be used to forecast time-series workload data, the authors were able to anticipate future workload demand and then proactively scale in both directions as needed. Unlike previous hybrid approaches that apply scaling sequentially, their method makes simultaneous scaling decisions to better balance resource allocation. They also implement a statistical burst detection mechanism to identify sudden spikes in the workload. 

One challenge that may arise is the lack of “exactly-once delivery guarantee” discussed in the MillWheel paper by Akidau et al. [2]. There, the authors found that in a low-latency fault-tolerant system, there will end up being duplication of data. To preemptively tackle this problem, the authors suggested that idempotent computations must be accompanied by persistent state management to prevent inaccurate data ingestion. 

As we scale up our simulated client requests, an additional challenge we may face is the oscillation of server provisions due to high latency spikes. As described in the Dhalion paper by Floratou et al. [3], we can monitor specific patterns such as backpressure or lagging customers to adjust resource allocation. Backpressure is a pattern of usage where slow consumers force the producers to slow down. Whereas lagging customers measures the gap between produced and consumed sequences, specifically for our research: MillisBehindLatest.  

## Open Areas of Research 
Several open areas of research remain in the design of scalable client-server distributed systems for real-time data processing. One major challenge is achieving “exactly-once” delivery semantics in high-throughput environments without introducing prohibitive latency or coordination overhead. Additionally, current autoscaling techniques, particularly in Kubernetes, rely heavily on resource-based metrics and fail to fully account for stream-specific indicators such as lag and backpressure, highlighting the need for more intelligent, latency-aware scaling strategies. While predictive autoscaling using machine learning shows promise, it remains an open problem to accurately model highly dynamic and bursty workloads such as user telemetry streams. Furthermore, efficient shard rebalancing and partition management in streaming systems continue to introduce latency spikes, indicating a need for more adaptive and seamless redistribution mechanisms. Other open challenges include robust backpressure handling across distributed pipelines, maintaining end-to-end Quality of Service (QoS), and improving observability and debugging tools for large-scale event-driven systems. These gaps present opportunities for future research and system-level innovations. 

## Discussion and Areas of Contribution
Our project implements a large distributed, real-time event-processing pipeline while utilizing the Client/Server Application Framework. We have meticulously chosen a Multi-tier Distributed Architecture to ensure isolation of concerns and independent scalability of each component. 

By leveraging Kinesis Shards, we address the numerical, geographical, and admin scale factor identified by Neuman. In our initial design phase, we hypothesized that vertical scaling increasing the CPU and RAM of our backend server nodes would be the most efficient way to handle traffic spikes due to the reduced overhead of internode communication; however, based on our research, we expect that a hybrid approach to scaling should outperform vertical scaling when under highly dynamic workloads.
